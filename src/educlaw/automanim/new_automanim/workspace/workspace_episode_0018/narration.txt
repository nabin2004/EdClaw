Let's see gradient clipping in action. We have two scenarios: unclipped gradients on the left, and clipped gradients on the right. Both start at the same position on this loss landscape. First, we compute the gradient at this point. The gradient value is negative 2.4, which is quite large. Now we apply gradient clipping with a threshold of 1.0. The unclipped gradient remains at negative 2.4, but the clipped version is bounded to negative 1.0. Watch what happens when we take the update step. The unclipped gradient causes a massive jump across the loss landscape, essentially exploding to the other side. In contrast, the clipped gradient produces a small, controlled step that moves smoothly toward the minimum. Without gradient clipping, large gradients cause training instability and exploding parameters. With clipping, we ensure bounded updates for stable convergence.
